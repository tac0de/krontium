# Krontium Reference Analysis

Status: planning draft, not canon.

This document records visual and research references for the redesign. References are evidence and inspiration, not project truth.

## Reference Method

For each reference, extract:

- composition
- material surface
- information hierarchy
- annotation style
- color behavior
- what Krontium should borrow
- what Krontium should avoid

## Primary Visual Axes

### 1. Natural History Plates

Reference:

- Ernst Haeckel, `Kunstformen der Natur`, Biodiversity Heritage Library: https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/bibliography/102214

Source notes:

- Published 1899-1904.
- BHL lists the work as public domain.
- Each plate has descriptive text.
- Useful for biological symmetry, specimen grouping, taxonomic framing, and non-human beauty.

What to borrow:

- structured plates
- repeated related forms
- numbered specimen logic
- fine linework around strange anatomy
- visual seriousness toward non-human life

What to avoid:

- copying ornamental symmetry so directly that gods feel decorative
- making the site look like a science poster instead of a fictional archive

Krontium use:

- Earth god dossier: root, bark, fungal networks, cambium scars as plate families
- Sky god dossier: feather structures, bone cross-sections, migration arcs
- Sea god dossier: chromatophore patterns, tentacle musculature, ink diffusion

### 2. Medieval Bestiary / Moral Natural History

References:

- British Library digitised manuscripts and archives: https://www.bl.uk/collection/digitised-manuscripts-archives
- Fordham Medieval Sourcebook, British Library Add MS 11283 bestiary: https://sourcebooks.web.fordham.edu/source/1180Medievalbestiary-BLAddMs11283.asp

Source notes:

- British Library presents digitised manuscripts and archival documents, including manuscript highlights and bestiary material.
- The Medieval Sourcebook notes that bestiaries describe real and legendary animals while also communicating moral meanings through allegory.
- The Add MS 11283 discussion is useful because it describes marginal notes, inserted titles, inconsistent entries, and a mixture of animal observation and theological meaning.

What to borrow:

- marginal authority
- conflicting observation and doctrine
- red/blue section marks
- simple but meaningful illustration placement
- text blocks that feel copied, translated, amended, and disputed

What to avoid:

- cute illuminated-manuscript fantasy styling
- direct Christian iconography unless transformed into Krontium's own theology
- excessive ornament that slows long-form reading

Krontium use:

- reader annotations
- disputed translation markers
- monk marginal notes
- dossier entries where a god's biological trait is followed by a religious interpretation

### 3. Medical and Anatomical Manuscripts

Reference:

- Wellcome Collection, Pseudo-Galen `Anatomia`: https://wellcomecollection.org/works/kn8vwcwq

Source notes:

- Wellcome lists the work as mid-15th century, online, with 134 images and Public Domain Mark.
- Description includes anatomical illustrations, skeletons, muscle figures, a pregnant woman, a wound man, marginal notes, and illuminated initials.

What to borrow:

- body as diagram
- wound-man logic
- annotations around injury and interpretation
- crude but authoritative medical drawing
- sacred discomfort around measuring bodies

What to avoid:

- gore as decoration
- modern medical cleanliness
- generic occult anatomy

Krontium use:

- "god wound" diagrams
- bark measurement scene
- divine autopsy taboo
- human military exploitation of observed anatomy

### 4. War Maps and Operational Records

Reference:

- Library of Congress Civil War Maps: https://www.loc.gov/collections/civil-war-maps/about-this-collection/

Source notes:

- LOC describes reconnaissance maps, sketch maps, coastal charts, theater-of-war maps, troop positions and movements, engagements, and fortifications.
- The collection includes maps from diaries, scrapbooks, manuscripts, atlases, and engineering records.

What to borrow:

- campaign routes
- troop position overlays
- fortification diagrams
- road/bridge/river hierarchy
- handwritten corrections
- operational uncertainty

What to avoid:

- treating maps as wallpaper
- fantasy maps with only decorative coastlines and names
- clean modern GIS style

Krontium use:

- War Atlas section
- reader chapter sidebars showing where a scene sits in a campaign
- human road network expansion
- neutral zone logistics

### 5. Scientific Anchors

References:

- Mycorrhizal symbiosis and land plants: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-018-28920-x
- Natural History Museum on dinosaurs to birds: https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/how-dinosaurs-evolved-into-birds.html
- Cephalopod brains overview: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6062618/
- Human cumulative culture: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8666907/

What to borrow:

- not literal exposition, but constraints
- gods should have ecological and anatomical consequences
- humans should become dangerous through cumulative social learning

What to avoid:

- lecturing the reader with science
- turning gods into mere animals
- using scientific names as decorative jargon

Krontium use:

- worldbuilding checks
- dossier fields
- warfare implications
- artifact labels

## Redesign Translation

### Landing

Replace abstract rings with an "Index Slab":

- a central stone/vellum catalog surface
- three partially visible specimen plates
- language editions as seals or catalog tabs
- first action: select edition or open first chronicle

### God Dossier

Each god gets a plate layout:

- specimen mark
- observed trait
- theological reading
- war implication
- disputed note
- related chapter

### Reader

Reader should look like a translation witness:

- chapter title
- source note
- provenance
- body text
- side notes on desktop
- collapsible notes on mobile
- no decorative cards

### War Atlas

Atlas should emphasize operations:

- routes
- rivers
- forts
- harvest boundaries
- supply
- disease
- disputed frontiers

## Visual Rule

Every visual element must answer at least one of these:

- What did the monks observe?
- What did a cult believe?
- What did an army exploit?
- What did humans learn faster than everyone else?

If it answers none, remove it.
