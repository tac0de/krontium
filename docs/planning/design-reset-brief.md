# Krontium Design Reset Brief

Status: planning draft, not canon.

## Current Prototype Diagnosis

The current site proves deployment and routing, but it does not yet express the novel's real identity.

Problems:

- The visual system is too generic: dark background, large title, abstract rings, and cards feel like a template.
- The gods are named but not embodied. The reader does not feel soil, feather, bone, salt, ink, root, rot, migration, or deep-sea intelligence.
- The site says "archive" but does not behave like a convincing archive. There are no catalog numbers, marginalia, provenance, redactions, disputed translations, specimen plates, or conflicting monk annotations.
- War and history are mentioned but not spatialized. There is no map logic, campaign pressure, border, supply route, season, or human institutional acceleration.
- The multilingual experience is functional but not conceptually meaningful. The languages should feel like parallel editions or translation witnesses, not just four buttons.

## Core Experience Target

Krontium should feel like opening a forbidden historical research interface maintained by a neutral monastic order.

The site is not a fantasy poster. It is a recovered dossier.

Primary reader fantasy:

> I am reading a translated archive that may be more accurate than the religions and empires inside the story want it to be.

## Design Pillars

### 1. Sacred Biology

The gods should be presented like living evolutionary contradictions, not decorative deities.

- Earth: root section, cambium rings, fungal networks, bark scars, soil strata, seed dispersal, land colonization.
- Sky: hollow bone diagrams, feather barbs, egg records, migration geometry, raptor posture, thermal maps.
- Sea: chromatophore fields, tentacle musculature, distributed nerve diagrams, abyssal pressure records, ink dispersal.

Visual vocabulary:

- specimen plates
- anatomical overlays
- field sketches
- fossil rubbings
- taxonomic tables
- monastic theological objections in margins

### 2. Historical Friction

The world should feel historically constrained.

War should be shown through:

- harvest cycles
- marching distance
- river crossings
- disease
- metallurgy
- fortification
- siege supply
- treaty witnesses
- casualty ledgers

Humans should feel weak but dangerous because they compress learning into institutions.

### 3. Monastic Neutrality

The neutral zone is the website's framing device.

The site can be presented as the archive of a cloister that:

- preserves all languages
- compares contradictory sources
- labels uncertainty
- annotates doctrine versus observation
- records battlefield evidence without serving any kingdom

This gives the interface a reason to exist.

### 4. Multilingual Witnesses

Languages should not feel like UI localization only.

Possible framing:

- English: modern translated edition
- Korean: primary author edition
- Chinese: classical court archive edition
- Japanese: scholastic annotated edition

This does not need to become story canon yet, but the UI can imply that each language is a different witness layer.

## Proposed Site Structure

### Landing: The Index Slab

First viewport:

- no marketing copy
- a large archival slab or specimen table
- three god plates partially visible
- language editions shown as archive seals
- immediate feeling of excavation, classification, and danger

Primary actions:

- choose edition
- open first chronicle
- inspect gods

### Edition Home

Purpose: orient the reader.

Sections:

- current translation note
- reading order
- three god dossiers
- neutral cloister summary
- war chronology preview
- human acceleration thesis

### Reader

Purpose: long-form prose first.

Features:

- strong typography
- chapter provenance note
- side annotations on desktop
- collapsible notes on mobile
- glossary terms that open as small archive entries
- disputed translation marks

### Atlas

Purpose: world history, not decorative map.

Features:

- neutral zone
- campaign routes
- ecological regions
- god cult territories
- human road network expansion over time

### Dossiers

Purpose: show the three gods as study objects.

Features:

- specimen plate
- biological observation
- religious interpretation
- historical consequence
- known cults
- war relevance

## Visual Direction Options

### Option A: Cloister Specimen Archive

Most fitting for the current premise.

Mood:

- restrained
- academic
- tactile
- unsettling

Materials:

- vellum
- blackened bronze
- bone ink
- salt-stained paper
- stone catalog tabs

Best for:

- serious long-form reading
- grounded history
- multilingual archive

Risk:

- can become too quiet if not enough god imagery is present

### Option B: War Ledger Interface

More military and historical.

Mood:

- campaign room
- casualty records
- borders under stress
- logistics-first history

Best for:

- making humans' rise feel believable
- showing geopolitical stakes

Risk:

- gods may become background lore instead of mythic pressure

### Option C: Sacred Evolutionary Codex

More visually distinctive and stranger.

Mood:

- illuminated manuscript meets natural history
- gods as evolutionary impossibilities
- theology fighting biology

Best for:

- memorable identity
- three-god visual language

Risk:

- can become too ornamental if not grounded by war and archives

## Recommended Direction

Blend A and C, with B as a structural layer.

In plain terms:

> The site should look like a neutral monastery's forbidden biological archive, built on top of war records.

## Immediate Redesign Goals

1. Replace abstract hero rings with a stronger "Index Slab" composition.
2. Make the gods visually legible as three biological systems.
3. Remove generic cards; use archive records, ledgers, specimen plates, and marginal notes.
4. Add a real reader layout with annotations and provenance.
5. Add a route/link integrity check before every deploy.

## Open Questions

1. Is the neutral monastic order the official frame narrator of the website?
2. Should the first page prioritize reading chapter 1, or exploring the archive first?
3. Are the three gods worshipped by separate civilizations, or do all civilizations interpret all three differently?
4. Are humans a late species biologically, a politically young species, or both?
5. Should the site hide advanced lore until the reader progresses, or expose the archive freely?
