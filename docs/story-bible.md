# Krontium Story Bible

Status: planning draft. This is not final canon until explicitly accepted.

## Core Identity

Krontium is a grounded epic fantasy about a world where gods are not distant abstractions. They have bodies, ecological pressure, biological traces, cults, enemies, and historical consequences.

The story should read like history under theological stress:

- people record what they can measure
- priests argue over what measurement means
- armies exploit what doctrine refuses to see
- humans survive because they learn faster than stronger species

## Framing Device

The website and, potentially, parts of the novel are framed through the archive of a neutral monastic territory.

Working name: The Neutral Cloister.

The Cloister does not claim perfect truth. It preserves conflicting witnesses:

- battlefield reports
- translated chronicles
- biological observations
- temple hymns
- tax records
- treaty copies
- heresy trials
- specimen drawings
- marginal objections by later monks

This frame lets the website present Krontium as a recovered dossier rather than a promotional page.

## The Three Ancient Gods

The gods are divine in religion, biological in trace, and political in consequence.

### Earth God

Working title: Root-Mantled Earth.

Form:

- tree-human silhouette
- bark skin
- root musculature
- cambium-ring wounds
- fungal/mycorrhizal network traces
- seed, spore, and soil imagery

Scientific anchor:

- early land plant colonization
- plant-fungus symbiosis
- soil formation
- decay as memory
- slow adaptation

Religious interpretation:

- god of patience, inheritance, burial, oath, and land-right
- associated with dynasties, ancestral law, boundary stones, grave groves

Historical pressure:

- controls fertility of land indirectly through cult, taboo, and ecological knowledge
- wars against this god's followers become wars of burning roots, poisoning orchards, blocking water, and breaking inheritance claims

Narrative danger:

- not rage, but persistence
- its wounds become forests
- killing its priests may not matter if the soil remembers their rites

### Sky God

Working title: Bone-Winged Sky.

Form:

- bird-human silhouette
- hollow bones
- feathered arms
- raptor posture
- egg, migration, and thermal imagery
- dinosaur-to-bird anatomical hints

Scientific anchor:

- theropod-to-bird evolution
- feathers before flight
- hollow/pneumatized bones
- migration, nesting, brooding, heat, altitude

Religious interpretation:

- god of witness, omen, kingship, distance, exposure, and execution
- its priests read flight paths, feather damage, shadows, and failed migrations

Historical pressure:

- favors courier networks, highland citadels, signal towers, surveillance, and seasonal warfare
- armies under this cult may move fast but suffer if supply and weather fail

Narrative danger:

- sees too much
- turns geography into judgment
- makes secrecy a sacrilege

### Sea God

Working title: Ink-Blooded Sea.

Form:

- octopus-human silhouette
- chromatophore skin
- soft intelligence
- distributed nerve system
- tentacular notation, ink, tide, abyssal pressure

Scientific anchor:

- cephalopod cognition
- distributed arm neurons
- camouflage and signaling
- problem solving
- intelligence without vertebrate structure

Religious interpretation:

- god of hidden speech, memory, trade, drowning, disguise, and treaty betrayal
- its priests treat color, silence, and pressure as forms of language

Historical pressure:

- dominates ports, espionage, hostage exchange, codes, and naval logistics
- sea wars are less about heroic fleets and more about grain, salt, customs, harbors, reefs, and missing records

Narrative danger:

- does not need a single mind to act
- remembers through fragments
- can make truth arrive as ink after the witness is dead

## Humans

Humans are not the strongest species.

They are weak in:

- hide
- claw
- lifespan
- sensory range
- divine inheritance
- resistance to cold, altitude, and disease

They become dangerous through compression of learning:

- writing
- standard measures
- metallurgy
- roads
- drill
- accounting
- law
- taxation
- siegecraft
- translation
- military schools
- institutional memory

Human advantage is cumulative culture. One human dies weak; a human institution remembers the error.

## The Neutral Cloister

The Cloister exists because every power needs records it cannot trust its own priests to keep.

Functions:

- translation house
- hostage monastery
- treaty witness
- specimen archive
- plague ledger
- map room
- war casualty registry
- burial arbitration office

Rules:

- no army may enter with banners
- no god's cult may destroy another cult's records
- no translation may erase its source witness
- no corpse from a treaty field may be buried without being counted

Contradiction:

The Cloister is neutral, but neutrality makes it powerful. Everyone hates its records when those records become useful to enemies.

## War Model

Wars should feel historical, not purely spectacular.

Useful constraints:

- harvest windows decide campaign timing
- rivers decide operational tempo
- roads decide state power
- winter decides whether cavalry is prestige or liability
- disease kills more quietly than monsters
- siege food matters more than courage
- gods change doctrine, but logistics decides how doctrine moves

Battle scenes should be grounded in:

- reconnaissance
- weather
- misread maps
- tax pressure
- deserters
- camp followers
- ferries
- mills
- salt
- horses or their absence
- supply fraud

## First Book Shape

Working structure:

1. The Cloister receives a disputed battlefield specimen: bark from a fallen earth idol.
2. Human officers treat the specimen as evidence that divine bodies can be studied.
3. Earth cult kingdoms call this sacrilege and mobilize inheritance-bound vassals.
4. Sky cult observers detect troop movements before declarations of war are issued.
5. Sea cult ports manipulate grain and information, deciding which war will starve first.
6. The Cloister becomes the only place where all three sides' records meet.
7. Humans win an early tactical victory by exploiting a biological fact, then misunderstand its moral cost.

## Website Implications

The site should expose the story through archive artifacts:

- The Index Slab: language and edition entry
- God Dossiers: sacred biology plates
- Cloister Ledger: chronology and disputed records
- War Atlas: routes, seasons, supply, and political pressure
- Reader: translated chronicles with marginal notes

The site should avoid telling the user "this is immersive." It should simply behave like an archive.
